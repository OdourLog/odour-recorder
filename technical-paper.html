1 · Purpose and scope

The Odour Intensity Recorder is a self-contained web application designed to support consultants and regulatory officers conducting structured field-based odour observations. It implements the methodology set out in the Institute of Air Quality Management's Guidance on the Assessment of Odour for Planning [1], producing a complete CSV record of a five-minute observation including per-sample intensities, derived statistical outputs, meteorological conditions, hedonic classification, surveyor identity and (where available) GPS coordinates.

This document describes the methodology adopted, the derivation logic implemented, the lookup tables consulted, and the assumptions and limitations that bear on the output. It is intended as the primary methodological reference for users of the tool and for downstream parties (planning officers, environmental health officers, expert witnesses) who receive the CSV outputs and need to understand how the figures were arrived at.

In scope

  • Structured five-minute field observation with thirty samples at ten-second intervals.
  • Calculation of mean intensity (Imean) and percentage of time above the recognisability threshold (t≥4).
  • Determination of odour exposure and odour effect from the IAQM matrices, parameterised by receptor sensitivity.
  • Derivation of Pasquill–Gifford atmospheric stability category from time, location, wind speed and cloud cover.
  • Capture of hedonic tone (dominant and varied) and Dravnieks atlas descriptors.
  • Anomaly flagging during recording with optional post-recording notes.
  • Production of a single CSV record per session.

Out of scope

  • Olfactometric quantification by dynamic dilution (governed by EN 13725 [6]).
  • Atmospheric dispersion modelling.
  • Intake or processing of complaints data.
  • Continuous (24-hour or longer) observation. The tool produces a snapshot.
  • Cross-observer agreement or panel methods. The tool assumes a single trained observer.

2 · Methodology overview

The IAQM 2018 framework [1] classifies odour effect on a four-point ordinal scale (Negligible, Slight adverse, Moderate adverse, Substantial adverse) based on the combination of an odour exposure category and a receptor sensitivity classification. Exposure is itself determined from a continuous-quantity intensity rating (the FIDOL framework's I term) integrated against the proportion of time the odour is recognisable. The tool implements this by structured field sampling.

A session captures three layers of information:

  1. The per-sample timeline — thirty discrete observer ratings on a 0–6 scale, one every ten seconds across a five-minute window.
  2. The contextual record — surveyor identity, location label, GPS coordinates, meteorological conditions, on-site activities and surrounding-area activities.
  3. The character record — hedonic tone, hedonic variation note, and one or more descriptors with their hedonic scores from the Dravnieks atlas [5].

Pip-pip-pip-beep audio cues at t–3, t–2, t–1 and t seconds before each sample help the observer prepare and capture an instantaneous rather than retrospective rating. The intensity rating at each ten-second tick is taken to be the slider value at that moment.

3 · The 0–6 intensity scale

The intensity scale is the seven-point ordinal scale specified in IAQM 2018 [1]. The observer rates the perceived strength of the odour at each sample tick on the following scale:

ValueLabelDescription
0No odourNo odour when compared to the clean site.
1Slight / very weakProbably some doubt as to whether the odour is actually present.
2Slight / weakOdour is present but cannot be described using precise words or terms.
3DistinctOdour character is barely recognisable.
4StrongOdour character is easily recognisable.
5Very strongOdour is offensive — exposure would be considered undesirable.
6Extremely strongOdour is offensive — instinctive reaction would be to mitigate against further exposure.

The threshold at intensity 4 ("Strong / easily recognisable") is the operative cutoff for the percentage-time calculation in the IAQM exposure matrix. This threshold corresponds to the point at which the odour is not merely present but clearly characterisable by a non-trained observer, and is the basis on which the tool's t≥4 aggregate is computed.

4 · Per-sample capture and aggregate calculations

For each session of 300 seconds duration, the application captures N = 30 samples at 10-second intervals starting at t = 10 s. The set of intensity ratings is denoted x1, x2, …, x30, with each xi ∈ {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.

Mean intensity

The mean intensity is the arithmetic average of the thirty samples, rounded to the nearest integer to align with the discrete intensity scale used by the IAQM exposure matrix:

Imean = round( (1/N) ⋅ Σi=1N xi ) where N = 30 and xi is the observer's intensity rating at sample i.

Percentage time at recognisable intensity

The percentage of time the odour is at or above the recognisability threshold is:

t≥4 = round( 100 ⋅ |{ i : xi ≥ 4 }| / N )% The numerator is the count of samples meeting or exceeding intensity 4 (the "Strong" threshold).

Special cases

Two boundary conditions are applied before the IAQM exposure matrix is consulted:

  • If Imean = 0, the exposure is set to Negligible regardless of the percentage time. This handles the edge case where a transient odour pulse early in a session is averaged away.
  • If Imean = 1 and t≥4 ≤ 10%, the exposure is again set to Negligible. This handles cases where the observer detected only a faint odour for a small portion of the session, where treating it as a positive exposure would be disproportionate.

Outside these two cases the exposure is read from the IAQM matrix (Appendix B) by using Imean as the row index and a binned representation of t≥4 as the column index. The binning is given by:

Column indexRange of t≥4
00% ≤ t ≤ 10%
110% < t ≤ 20%
220% < t ≤ 30%
330% < t ≤ 40%
440% < t ≤ 100%

5 · Receptor sensitivity classification

The IAQM framework specifies three receptor sensitivity classes [1], reflecting the relative tolerance of the receiving land use to odour exposure. The classification is not derived by the tool — it is set by the surveyor based on the receptor in question and is a mandatory input on the setup screen:

ClassTypical receptors
HighResidential dwellings, hospitals, schools, tourist or cultural sites where the receptor's primary purpose involves prolonged occupation and a reasonable expectation of an absence of unpleasant odour.
MediumWorkplaces, commercial or retail premises, recreational fields where occupation is regular but secondary to the receptor's function, and a less acute expectation of low odour.
LowIndustrial sites, farms, footpaths, roads where occupation is transient and odour is often associated with the activity itself.

6 · Exposure determination

The odour exposure is one of four ordinal categories — Small, Medium, Large, Very Large — determined by indexing the IAQM exposure matrix by the rounded mean intensity (rows, descending from 6 to 1) and the binned percentage time (columns, ascending from 0 to 4). Two cells in the matrix are marked N/A because the IAQM framework does not consider the combination physically meaningful (intensity 1 with high percentage time is internally inconsistent).

The matrix is reproduced in full in Appendix B, Table B.1.

The two boundary conditions described in §4 take precedence over the matrix lookup:

  1. Imean = 0 ⇒ Negligible.
  2. Imean = 1 ∧ t≥4 ≤ 10% ⇒ Negligible.

The "Negligible" exposure category is therefore an additional outcome over and above the four matrix categories. It collapses to "Negligible" in the effect determination regardless of receptor sensitivity (see §7).

7 · Effect determination

The final IAQM odour effect classification is one of four categories: Negligible, Slight adverse, Moderate adverse, or Substantial adverse. It is determined from the exposure category and the receptor sensitivity by indexing the IAQM effect matrix.

The matrix is reproduced in full in Appendix B, Table B.2.

The principle embedded in the matrix is that the same exposure causes a more adverse effect at a more sensitive receptor. For example a "Medium" exposure produces a "Slight adverse" effect at a Low- or Medium-sensitivity receptor but a "Moderate adverse" effect at a High-sensitivity receptor.

Two special cases:

  • If exposure is Negligible, the effect is Negligible for all receptor sensitivities.
  • If exposure is marked N/A in the matrix (a physically inconsistent combination), the effect is also marked N/A.

8 · Atmospheric stability derivation

Atmospheric stability is captured using the Pasquill–Gifford classification scheme [2][3], which assigns one of six categories (A–F) describing the degree of vertical mixing in the lower atmosphere. Stability is required as a meteorological context for the survey but is not used in the IAQM effect calculation; it is recorded for downstream interpretation and dispersion comparison purposes.

ClassNameConditions
AVery unstableStrong daytime heating, light wind, plume loops and disperses rapidly.
BUnstableModerate-to-strong daytime heating with moderate wind, cumulus develops.
CSlightly unstableSlight daytime heating or strong heating with stronger wind.
DNeutralOvercast (day or night) or any strong wind. The most common condition in temperate climates and the most frequently observed UK condition.
ESlightly stableClear or partly cloudy night with light to moderate wind, plume fans.
FStableClear night with light wind, mist or fog often forming, plume hugs ground.

The tool implements an automated suggestion of the stability class through a "Suggest from sun + wind + cloud" action on the conditions screen. This combines three inputs: the time of day (used to compute solar elevation), the wind speed (converted from the observer's selected Beaufort band to a representative metres-per-second value) and the cloud cover bucket. The derivation runs in three stages.

Solar elevation

The solar elevation angle α (degrees above horizon) is computed using a NOAA-style approximation [4] from the calendar date, time, and latitude/longitude. The accuracy is approximately ±0.5° within the British Isles, more than adequate for the coarse insolation tiers required by the Pasquill–Gifford scheme. The full procedure:

  1. Compute day-of-year n from the supplied date.
  2. Compute the fractional year angle γ:
    γ = (2π / 365) ⋅ ( n − 1 + (h − 12) / 24 )where h is the local hour.
  3. Compute the solar declination δ from a truncated Fourier series in γ.
  4. Compute the equation of time E from a similar Fourier expansion.
  5. Compute the solar time, hour angle and finally the elevation:
    sin α = sin(φ) sin(δ) + cos(φ) cos(δ) cos(H)where φ is latitude and H is the hour angle in radians.

For the day/night cutoff the tool uses α > 3°. This is slightly above the geometric horizon to account for atmospheric refraction and to avoid the ambiguous low-elevation period when surface solar input is negligible but the sun is technically above the horizon.

Insolation tier (daytime)

The solar elevation is mapped to one of three insolation tiers, then adjusted downward by the cloud cover bucket. The tiers are:

Solar elevationInsolation
α > 60°Strong
35° < α ≤ 60°Moderate
3° < α ≤ 35°Slight

The cloud cover bucket reduces the insolation tier:

  • Clear (0–2 oktas) — no reduction.
  • Partly cloudy (3–5 oktas) — reduce insolation by one tier (Strong → Moderate, Moderate → Slight, Slight → unchanged at zero).
  • Mostly cloudy (6–7 oktas) — reduce by two tiers.
  • Overcast (8 oktas) — bypasses the lookup; daytime overcast yields class D directly.

Wind speed conversion

The Beaufort band selected by the observer is converted to a representative wind speed in m/s using the mid-band of each Beaufort range:

BeaufortDescriptionm/s (approx)
0Calm0
1Light air1
2Light breeze2
3Gentle breeze4
4Moderate breeze7
5Fresh breeze10
6Strong breeze12
7Near gale16
8+Gale20

Stability lookup

With insolation tier (or, for night, the cloud cover) and wind speed in m/s, the stability category is read from the lookup grid in Appendix A. Where the canonical Pasquill–Gifford table specifies an interpolated boundary (such as "A–B"), the implementation selects a single class deterministically — the more unstable class for daytime boundaries and the more stable class for night-time boundaries — to produce a single suggested output. The original Pasquill table values and the deterministic resolution rule are both shown in Appendix A.

Location source for the suggestion Because GPS coordinates are captured at recording start (after the conditions screen), the Suggest action runs before GPS is available. In this case the application uses a UK-centroid fallback latitude (54.0°N, 2.0°W). This produces a solar elevation that differs from the actual surveyor location by no more than ±0.5° for any UK observation, which is well below the granularity of the insolation tiers and never changes the resulting Pasquill–Gifford class. The rationale message identifies the location source explicitly so the surveyor can verify or override.

9 · Hedonic tone and descriptor capture

Hedonic tone (the surveyor's overall impression of pleasantness) and descriptor identification are captured after the recording phase. Both are mandatory inputs for the survey record, though the descriptor field accepts free text in lieu of, or in addition to, structured selections.

Dominant tone and variation

The dominant hedonic tone is recorded on a three-point scale: Pleasant, Neutral or Unpleasant. The surveyor selects one. A separate boolean flag, "Tone varied during session", is provided for cases where the character changed materially within the five-minute window — a typical example is a sweet-floral profile transitioning to a chemical or solvent profile. When ticked, an optional free-text field captures the description of the variation.

This pattern (single dominant + boolean varied + free text) was chosen over the alternative of capturing per-sample hedonic tones because:

  • The hedonic dimension is generally more stable than the intensity dimension within a single observation window.
  • Per-sample hedonic capture would impose substantial cognitive load on the observer alongside the per-sample intensity rating.
  • The boolean flag preserves the analytical signal (variation occurred / did not) for downstream analysis without requiring the granular detail.

Dravnieks descriptors

The application provides a search-and-select interface over 141 standard odour descriptors drawn from the Dravnieks (1985) Atlas of Odor Character Profiles [5]. Each descriptor is associated with a hedonic score (a continuous value in approximately the range −3.75 to +3.53) derived from panel ratings of the descriptor's pleasantness or unpleasantness. The descriptors are sorted with the most negative (most unpleasant) first, as regulatory and complaint-driven surveys are typically concerned with unpleasant sources.

The full list with scores is reproduced in Appendix C.

Multi-selection is supported. When more than one descriptor is selected, the application reports the arithmetic mean of the individual scores in the results screen and CSV, alongside the comma-separated list of selected descriptors and their individual scores. This provides a quantified summary for blended odour characters that no single descriptor captures.

An "Additional / alternative description" free-text field is provided for descriptors not in the standard list, or for nuance that the standard list cannot capture. The session can complete with this field alone (no Dravnieks selections) — a common case for site-specific odours such as rendering plant emissions, anaerobic digestate, or industry-specific solvents.

10 · Meteorological conditions

Six meteorological fields are mandatory and two are optional. The mandatory fields are those required by IAQM 2018 to qualify the survey context; the optional fields are atmospheric record items that are useful for cross-referencing but are sometimes genuinely unavailable to the surveyor in the field.

Wind strengthYesBeaufort 0–8+ (see §8 conversion table).
Cloud coverYesFour buckets: Clear (0–2 oktas), Partly cloudy (3–5 oktas), Mostly cloudy (6–7 oktas), Overcast (8 oktas).

Two free-text fields are also captured: On-site Operations & Activities describes what is happening at the site under observation (slurry spreading, kitchen exhaust, processing line running) and Surrounding Area Activities describes other potential odour sources nearby (neighbouring farms, traffic, vegetation). Neither is mandatory but both are persisted to the CSV when supplied.

11 · Anomaly flagging

An anomaly flag is a structured marker the surveyor can apply during recording to indicate that a specific sample window was contaminated by an unrelated event — a vehicle passing, an extractor cycling on, a door opening at a neighbouring property. The flag is an explicit acknowledgement that the affected sample's intensity rating may not represent the source under investigation.

Flags are captured by tapping a dedicated flag button on the recording screen. A 400 ms debounce is applied so that an accidental double-tap counts as one flag. Each flag captures:

  • The session-relative time at the moment of the tap (e.g. 0:11).
  • The slider's current intensity value.
  • An optional free-text note added on a post-recording review screen.

After the recording phase ends, if any flags were captured the application presents an Anomaly Review screen. Each flag is listed with its time and recorded intensity, and a free-text input is provided for the optional note. The notes screen is skip-able — the flag itself is the structured signal, and notes are nice-to-have detail.

In the CSV record, anomalies surface in two places:

  1. An Anomaly column on each per-sample row, set to true when the sample window contains a flag and otherwise empty.
  2. A separate Anomalies block at the end of the file listing each flag with its sequence number, time, intensity, and note.

The two-locations representation lets a downstream parser do either approach: filter the per-sample rows by the Anomaly column to exclude contaminated samples from re-analysis, or read the Anomalies block to retrieve narrative context for QA review.

12 · Output schema

Every session produces a single CSV file with a defined schema. The current schema is version 3, introduced in application v1.2.0; this number increments whenever the column structure or methodology changes in a way that could affect a downstream parser. Schema 3 extends Schema 2 with per-sample timing telemetry, visibility-loss tracking, and pause logging.

File structure

The CSV is logically composed of five to seven blocks separated by blank lines:

  1. Per-sample table at the top: thirty rows of Time, Seconds, Actual Time (s), Inter-tick Drift (s), Cumulative Drift (s), Vis Interrupt (s), Vis Interrupt (%), Intensity, Odour Strength, Status, Timing Flag, Anomaly.
  2. Summary block: key-value pairs with the schema/version metadata, timestamp, timezone, surveyor identity, location, GPS coordinates, IAQM outputs (or N/A if compromised), hedonic and descriptor records, and Schema 3 timing telemetry.
  3. Meteorological Conditions block: key-value pairs with the eight conditions fields.
  4. Site Operations block: the two free-text activity fields.
  5. Pauses block (only present if any pauses occurred): one row per pause with trigger, pause time, resume time, and duration.
  6. Anomalies block (only present if any flags were captured): one row per flag.

Per-sample columns

The twelve per-sample columns capture both the user-supplied intensity rating and the timing/visibility provenance of each capture:

  • Time, Seconds — nominal sample time as mm:ss and integer seconds (10, 20, … 300). Constant across all sessions; useful for joining or pivoting.
  • Actual Time (s) — the attentive elapsed seconds at the moment of capture, derived from performance.now() minus accumulated pause time. For a perfectly timed sample N, this equals 10×N. Empty for MISSED samples.
  • Inter-tick Drift (s) — signed difference between this sample's actual time and the previous sample's actual time, minus the nominal 10-second interval. For sample 1, the reference is recording start (attentive 0). Empty if the previous sample was MISSED or a manual pause occurred between them.
  • Cumulative Drift (s) — signed total drift since recording start: actualTime − nominalTime.
  • Vis Interrupt (s), Vis Interrupt (%) — number of real seconds the page was hidden during this sample's 10-second window, expressed both absolutely and as a percentage of the window. Capped at 10s.
  • Intensity, Odour Strength — the surveyor's rating and its text label. Empty for MISSED.
  • Status — MISSED if the sample's nominal capture moment fell within a visibility-hidden episode (boundary rule: ev.startReal ≤ realAtCapture ≤ ev.endReal); otherwise empty.
  • Timing Flag — true if |Inter-tick Drift| exceeded 1.0 second (10% of the nominal interval) or the sample is MISSED.
  • Anomaly — true if the sample window contained a surveyor-flagged anomaly.

Summary block additions (Schema 3)

The following keys are added to the Summary block beyond Schema 2:

  • Timezone — IANA zone derived from Intl.DateTimeFormat().resolvedOptions().timeZone.
  • Recording Duration (s) — total wall-clock recording time from start to end. Equals 300 for a clean recording; longer if any pauses occurred.
  • Overall Drift (s), Overall Drift (%) — signed difference between attentive elapsed time at end of recording and 300 seconds, expressed in seconds and as a percentage. Excludes pause time.
  • Visibility Interruptions — count of distinct hidden-then-visible episodes during recording.
  • Total Hidden Time (s), Total Hidden Time (%) — sum of all visibility-hidden duration, absolute and as a percentage of Recording Duration.
  • Compromised Samples — count of samples flagged as compromised. A sample is compromised if it is MISSED or its 10-second window overlaps any visibility episode lasting more than 10 seconds.
  • MISSED Samples — count of samples whose nominal capture moment fell within a hidden episode. Always ≤ Compromised Samples.
  • Pauses — total count of pause events (visibility plus manual).
  • Manual Pause Time (s) — sum of explicit Pause-button durations.
  • Timing Status — one of OK, Drift detected, Compromised, or Drift detected; compromised. Drives the yellow banner on the results screen and signals downstream consumers whether the timing telemetry warrants review.
  • Device — parsed device fingerprint (e.g. iPhone; iOS 17.4; Safari 17.4) extracted from the navigator.userAgent string. Used by support to triage device-specific timing issues.

If Compromised Samples > 0, the IAQM-related rows (Average Intensity, Percentage Odour Time, Odour Exposure, Odour Effect) take the literal value N/A rather than computed numbers. The CSV is still saved with all telemetry preserved so a reviewer can decide whether a manual recomputation against the valid samples is appropriate.

Pauses block

Present only if at least one pause occurred. Five columns: Pause #, Trigger, Pause Time, Resume Time, Duration (s). Trigger values:

  • Visibility — the page entered the hidden state. The recorded duration includes both the hidden episode and any subsequent live-abort modal display time as a single combined entry, since the recording timer is paused for both.
  • Manual — the surveyor tapped the Pause button. Modal display time for the manual pause overlay is included in the duration.

Pause Time and Resume Time are mm:ss offsets from the recording start (real time, not attentive). Entries are listed in chronological order.

Quoting and escaping

The file uses RFC 4180 quoting throughout. Any value containing a comma, double-quote, or newline is wrapped in double quotes; embedded double-quotes are escaped by doubling. This applies uniformly to all user-supplied free-text fields (location name, surveyor name, on-site activities, surrounding area activities, additional descriptor, anomaly notes) and protects against accidentally introducing additional columns or breaking row boundaries.

Schema versioning

Two version-related rows appear at the top of the Summary block:

  • App Version — semver string identifying the application build (e.g. 1.2.0). Cosmetic and behavioural changes that do not affect the schema bump the application version but not the schema.
  • Schema — integer identifying the column structure and methodology version (e.g. 3). Any change that adds/removes/reorders columns, or changes the methodology in a way that affects calculated values, increments the schema number.

Downstream parsers should branch on the Schema value when supporting multiple historical formats. Schema 3 is a strict superset of Schema 2 in the per-sample table (additional columns appended in fixed positions), so a Schema 2 parser that reads only the first five per-sample columns will continue to function on Schema 3 files but will miss the timing telemetry.

Filename convention

The default filename is constructed from the location name and the survey start timestamp:

{Location Name} {YYYY-MM-DD} {HH-MM}.csv

For example: Plant 3 North Boundary 2026-04-28 17-44.csv. The hyphen separator in the time portion (rather than the colon used inside the CSV's Time field) is required because colons are not valid filename characters on Windows or iOS. The user can override the filename before download.

13 · Coordinate capture

GPS coordinates are captured at the moment the surveyor taps "Start Recording" on the conditions screen, immediately before the timer begins. This is implemented through the browser's standard Geolocation API.

The acquisition parameters are:

  • enableHighAccuracy: true — requests the device's most precise positioning available, which on iOS triggers GNSS rather than coarse Wi-Fi triangulation when conditions permit.
  • timeout: 8000 — the application abandons the request after eight seconds and proceeds without coordinates.
  • maximumAge: 60000 — a position fix obtained within the previous 60 seconds may be reused.

Three outcomes are possible:

  1. Success — the position is recorded with its reported accuracy (in metres) and the session begins.
  2. Permission denied — the application presents a confirmation dialog ("Location permission denied. Continue without GPS coordinates?") and the surveyor can choose whether to abandon or to proceed without coordinates.
  3. Timeout or error — same dialog flow as permission denied.

When GPS coordinates are present, three rows appear in the CSV Summary: Latitude, Longitude and GPS Accuracy (m), with the latitude and longitude recorded to six decimal places (approximately 11 cm precision) and the accuracy to integer metres. When GPS is absent, the rows are present but empty.

14 · Persistence and session state

Surveyor name retention

The surveyor name is persisted in the browser's localStorage on the device, scoped to the application origin. This means a surveyor entering their name once does not re-enter it for subsequent sessions on the same device. A different surveyor using the same device can simply edit the name on the setup screen, and the new value is saved. This is the only piece of state the application persists across sessions.

Mid-session persistence

The application does not persist in-progress sessions across browser reloads or device restarts. If the device restarts during the five-minute recording phase, the data captured up to that point is unrecoverable. The recommended practice is to keep the application in the foreground until the CSV is saved.

Wake lock

For the duration of the recording phase, the application requests a screen wake lock through the browser's Wake Lock API [7]. This prevents the device's screen from dimming or sleeping, which would otherwise pause the JavaScript timer and cause samples to be missed. The wake lock is released on session completion (whether the timer expires naturally, the surveyor ends the session early via the Pause overlay, or recording is aborted). On platforms or browser versions that do not support the Wake Lock API the request silently no-ops, but a screen sleep would still be observable as a session interruption (and would now also be captured as a visibility-loss episode in the per-sample telemetry).

Timing and visibility tracking (Schema 3)

The recorder anchors all timing measurements to performance.now(), a monotonic high-resolution clock unaffected by wall-clock corrections (NTP adjustments, manual clock changes, daylight savings). At the moment of each sample capture, the difference between performance.now() and the recording start time, minus any accumulated pause time, gives the attentive elapsed seconds. The signed difference between this value and the nominal capture time produces the cumulative drift; the difference between successive samples' attentive elapsed values, minus the nominal 10-second interval, produces the inter-tick drift.

The Page Visibility API [10] tracks every transition into and out of the hidden state via document.visibilityState and the visibilitychange event. A hidden episode is recorded as a {startReal, endReal, durationS} tuple. On return to the visible state, the application evaluates two thresholds — single-episode duration > 10 seconds, or cumulative hidden time since the last warning > 15 seconds — and fires a "Survey may be compromised" modal if either is crossed. The modal pauses the recording timer (cancels the pending tick and prevents new ticks from firing) until the surveyor selects Continue or Abort, ensuring the surveyor's deliberation time is not itself counted against subsequent samples.

The boundary rule for MISSED status uses inclusive comparison on both ends: a sample whose realAtCapture falls within [ev.startReal, ev.endReal] for any visibility episode ev is marked MISSED. The strict ≤ on both ends reflects the fact that a sample fired at the exact moment of visibility transition still gives the surveyor zero attentive time to review or amend the slider value.

The compromised-sample definition combines this with a window-overlap rule: a sample is compromised if it is MISSED or its 10 attentive seconds (mapped to real time) overlap any visibility episode of duration > 10 seconds. The 10-second threshold for the overlap rule is methodologically equivalent to the live-modal trigger — any visibility loss large enough to fire a modal is treated as compromising the affected sample(s).

Manual pauses (initiated by the surveyor tapping the Pause button) follow a separate code path that does not generate MISSED or Compromised status. A manual pause is by definition a deliberate, witnessed break in the recording, recorded in the Pauses block as a Manual entry with full duration. The recording resumes from the point of pause, with the next sample firing 10 attentive seconds later.

15 · Assumptions and limitations

The methodology and implementation rely on a number of assumptions, and the resulting record carries corresponding limitations. These should be understood by anyone commissioning, conducting, or relying on the output of a survey.

Single-observer methodology

The tool assumes a single observer. There is no cross-observer agreement or panel reliability check built in. Inter-observer variation in odour intensity rating is well-documented in the literature and the tool's record reflects the single observer's assessment only. For applications requiring panel-style assessment, dynamic olfactometry to EN 13725 [6] is the appropriate methodology.

Snapshot, not continuous monitoring

A five-minute observation is a snapshot. Odour exposure varies with operational state, meteorology, and time of day. A single session captures conditions over five minutes only and cannot speak to longer-term temporal patterns. For a defensible characterisation of a receptor's odour environment, multiple sessions across time of day, day of week, season and meteorological conditions are typically required. The tool's CSV format and timestamped filename convention are designed to support such a programme of sessions.

Slider linearity

The intensity scale is ordinal, not interval. Computing an arithmetic mean over an ordinal scale is methodologically conventional within IAQM 2018 but assumes that the surveyor interprets adjacent values as approximately equally spaced. Surveyor calibration through training and reference exposures is the standard mitigation.

Browser runtime constraints

The application runs in a web browser on the surveyor's device, predominantly iOS Safari in field deployments. Three iOS-specific behaviours bear on data integrity:

  • Audio cues will not play until the user has performed at least one tap (the audio context is unlocked by the Start Recording tap). This is a fixed iOS Safari constraint and cannot be worked around.
  • If the application is backgrounded (the user switches apps or locks the device) during recording, iOS Safari throttles or suspends the JavaScript timer. As of Schema 3, every visibility transition is captured via the Page Visibility API and surfaced both in real time as a warning modal (when a threshold is crossed) and in the CSV as per-sample Vis Interrupt values, a Pauses block entry, and a Compromised Samples count. The Wake Lock implementation reduces the likelihood of unintended backgrounding but does not eliminate it.
  • iOS Safari's quiet hours and silent mode override the application's audio output. A surveyor who switches to silent mode will not hear the pip-pip-pip-beep cues; the timer continues to run and capture samples, but the audible structure of the session is lost.

GPS accuracy

Reported accuracy varies from a few metres in clear sky GNSS conditions to several tens of metres in urban canyons or indoor surveys. The accuracy value is preserved in the CSV so downstream consumers can apply their own confidence thresholds.

Solar elevation approximation

The solar elevation calculation is accurate to approximately ±0.5°, which is well within the granularity of the Pasquill–Gifford insolation tiers (boundaries at 35° and 60°). The approximation introduces no observable error in the stability category derivation.

Cloud cover bucketing

The four-bucket cloud cover scheme abstracts the eighths-of-sky (oktas) standard into a coarser categorisation that is more reliable for an observer estimating by eye than asking for a numeric oktas value. The trade-off is loss of granularity at the bucket boundaries; a sky at the boundary between Partly cloudy and Mostly cloudy may be classified differently by different observers, which can swing the suggested stability class by one tier near the day-edge cases.

Pre-recording PG suggestion

As described in §8, the Suggest action runs before GPS is available and uses a UK-centroid fallback latitude. This is methodologically defensible for any UK observation but would introduce systematic error if the application were used outside the British Isles. Surveyors operating elsewhere should manually set the stability category rather than relying on the suggestion.

Fixed descriptor list

The Dravnieks atlas descriptor list is fixed at the 141 entries from the 1985 publication [5]. It is not user-extensible. Site-specific descriptors not in the list (e.g. "rendering plant," "pulper effluent") must be captured through the free-text additional description field.

Background dispersion

The tool records what the observer perceives at the survey point. It does not account for source-to-receptor dispersion, atmospheric back-trajectory analysis, or apportionment between possible sources. These are dispersion-modelling exercises outside the scope of an in-situ observation.

16 · Quality assurance considerations

The output CSV is designed to support a defensible audit trail.

  • Every session carries the surveyor's identity, the start timestamp, GPS coordinates with reported accuracy, and the application and schema versions.
  • Every per-sample rating is preserved verbatim alongside the aggregate calculations, so a third party can recompute the IAQM outputs from the raw data and confirm the matrix lookups.
  • Anomaly flags provide explicit acknowledgement of contamination events with their time and the surveyor's note.
  • The schema versioning and RFC 4180 quoting protect against accidental data corruption from edge-case free-text inputs.

What the tool does not provide:

  • Olfactometric quantification (concentration in odour units per cubic metre). This requires controlled laboratory dilution to EN 13725 [6], which is outside the scope of any in-situ observation.
  • Statistical inference about a receptor's typical exposure. A single session is one data point; a representative characterisation requires a programme of sessions designed to sample the relevant variability.
  • Compliance determination against any specific regulatory standard. The tool computes the IAQM 2018 outputs as the methodology defines them; whether those outputs constitute compliance under any given local regulation, planning condition, or environmental permit is a separate question that depends on the regulatory framework in question.
