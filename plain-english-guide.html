A plain-language guide

Odour surveys, in plain English

What a surveyor was doing during those five minutes outside your home or business — and what the result actually tells you.

If a surveyor recently spent five minutes outside your home or business with a phone in their hand, this explains what they were doing. If you've commissioned a survey and have a report in front of you, this explains what the result means.

The aim here is to give you enough to understand the work, without the jargon. There's a separate technical paper covering the detailed methodology if you want it.

What happened during those five minutes

The surveyor stood in one fixed place for exactly five minutes. Every ten seconds, they rated the smell at that moment on a scale from 0 to 6. They were using their phone to keep the timing precise — it makes three short pips and then a longer beep at every ten-second mark, so they don't have to keep watching a clock.

While they were rating, they also noted down the wind direction and strength, the cloud cover, the temperature, and anything unusual that happened during the survey — a tractor passing by, a delivery van idling, a cooking smell drifting from a nearby kitchen. These get flagged so they can be reviewed afterwards.

After the five minutes were over, they answered a few more questions. Was the smell pleasant, neutral, or unpleasant overall? What did it actually smell like, chosen from a standard list of 141 descriptions? All of this is written into a single record, which becomes the basis for the assessment.

The 0 to 6 scale

The seven points on the scale aren't arbitrary numbers. Each one corresponds to a specific perceptual moment that the surveyor is trained to recognise:

  1. 0No odourThe air smells like clean air. Nothing.
  2. 1Slight or very weakYou're not sure if there's a smell or you're imagining it.
  3. 2Slight or weakDefinitely something there, but you couldn't put a name to it.
  4. 3DistinctYou can just about tell what it is.
  5. 4StrongYou can clearly tell what it is.
  6. 5Very strongStrong enough that you'd want to get away from it.
  7. 6Extremely strongStrong enough that your instinctive reaction is to leave.

A useful way to picture the scale is to think of brightness. 0 is total darkness; 6 is looking at the sun on a clear day. The scale asks the surveyor to position the smell at a corresponding rung between nothing and overwhelming.

The point at which the surveyor can clearly tell what they're smelling — level 4, "strong" — is an important threshold. Below that, the smell might be there but isn't really characterisable. From 4 upwards, you can tell what it is. The assessment cares particularly about how much of the survey time was at or above this threshold.

Two numbers that matter

From the thirty samples, two summary numbers come out:

  • The average intensity — the mean of all thirty ratings, rounded. A survey with mostly 3s and 4s might average to a 3.
  • The percentage of time at intensity 4 or above — the share of samples where the smell was clearly recognisable.

Both numbers matter because the experience of a smell depends on both how strong it gets and how often it reaches that strength. A faint smell that's there constantly is a different experience from a strong smell that comes and goes. A strong smell that's there most of the time is worse than either. The assessment combines both.

What the result means

The two numbers combine to give an exposure rating: Small, Medium, Large, or Very Large. That rating is then combined with the kind of place the survey was done — a home, a hospital or a school is treated as more sensitive than a factory or a farm — because the same exposure has different consequences depending on where it lands.

The final result is one of four categories:

  • NegligibleThe smell is there but, taking into account both how strong it gets and how often it's recognisable, it isn't expected to cause meaningful disturbance.
  • Slight adverseThe smell is noticeable enough to be a minor nuisance from time to time. Most people in most circumstances would carry on without serious disruption.
  • Moderate adverseThe smell is significant. People living or working at the location would be expected to notice it regularly and to find it unpleasant. This is the level at which planning decisions usually start to require active mitigation.
  • Substantial adverseThe smell is severe. Living or working at the location would be considered seriously affected, and significant action to reduce the smell is normally required.

These four categories are nationally consistent. The same survey method, the same scale, and the same combination tables produce the same category whether the survey is done in Cornwall, Cumbria, or anywhere else in the UK. That consistency is the point. It's why the result can be defended in a planning case, a permit application, or an investigation of a complaint.

Why these particular methods?

The methodology is the standard set out by the Institute of Air Quality Management, the recognised UK body for air quality professionals. Their 2018 guidance is what regulators, planning inspectors, and consultants all work to. Following it means the result is comparable across surveys, surveyors, and projects.

The atmospheric stability part of the survey — the note about whether the air was unstable, neutral or stable that day — comes from the Pasquill-Gifford classification, a system used worldwide since the 1960s for understanding how plumes of anything (smoke, smell, pollutants) spread out. The simplest way to picture it: on a still, clear night, smells travel a long way without diluting, a bit like ink dropped into still water. On a windy, bright day, they get rapidly broken up and diluted, like ink dropped into a stirred glass. The stability category captures which of those regimes was active during the survey, which matters for understanding the result.

The list of smell descriptions — Bakery, Faecal, Sulphurous, Floral, and so on — is the Dravnieks atlas, a standard reference list of 141 odour descriptions published by the American Society for Testing and Materials in 1985. It exists so that different surveyors describe the same smell the same way. Without a standard list, one surveyor might write "rotting", another "decayed", another "off", and the assessment loses consistency. The atlas fixes that.

What this tells you, and what it doesn't

A single five-minute survey is a snapshot. It says, with some confidence, what the smell was doing at that one place, at that one time. That is genuinely useful — the result is a carefully gathered, defensible record that feeds into a wider assessment.

It does not tell you:

  • What it's like at other times. A survey done in dry, calm conditions might come back as Negligible while a survey done in a different wind direction, or after a different process activity at the source, might come back as Moderate. A full picture usually needs several surveys, ideally in different conditions.
  • What it's like at your home day-to-day, hours after the surveyor has left. Your own experience builds up over dozens of hours per week. The survey is one five-minute window.
  • What the source actually is. Even with a confident odour description, the survey alone doesn't prove which facility or process is responsible. That requires additional investigation.
  • How concentrated the smell is in chemical terms. That's a different measurement (called olfactometry) made in a laboratory using a collected air sample. It's a separate kind of evidence, used for different purposes.

These limits don't make the survey less useful. They define what it is and isn't. The result — Negligible, Slight, Moderate, or Substantial adverse — is one carefully gathered piece of evidence in a wider picture. Read alongside other surveys, complaint records, and where they exist dispersion modelling and chemical measurements, it contributes to a defensible conclusion. Read on its own, it answers a narrower but still real question: what was the smell doing at this place, at this time, against a national standard scale?

If you have questions

If you've received a result and aren't sure what it means in your particular case, the right person to ask is whoever commissioned the work or carried out the survey. They'll know the wider context — how many surveys were done, what conditions they sampled, what the assessment as a whole concluded — that a single survey result on its own can't capture.

If something in this guide has raised a question that the consultant or assessor can't answer to your satisfaction, your local council's environmental health team is the next port of call. They have powers to investigate odour complaints and to require action where it's needed.
